A Matrix Approach to Least Squares

2020-08-26

The least squares method is a common approach to regression analysis in approximating solutions to overdetermined equations. From a statistical perspective, it provides a method to find lines of best fit to a given data set. The method of least squares has vast applications in the fields of linear regression, machine learning, and data analysis. While typical least squares approaches rely on techniques from calculus, today we will examine a matrix approach to the least squares method. Through this introduction, we will target a number of linear algebra topics, including orthogonality, projections, and matrix algebra.


Some Background: Orthogonality

Two vectors, u⃗\vec{u} u and v⃗\vec{v} v are said to be orthogonal if their dot product is 000. For the purposes of consistency, we will denote the dot product of two vectors in inner product notation: ⟨u⃗,v⃗⟩\langle \vec{u} , \vec{v} \rangle⟨u,v⟩. (It can be easily shown that the dot product of two vectors is zero if and only if the two vectors are orthogonal using the Law of Cosines.)


A Simpler Problem

Before we move any farther, let’s first consider a simpler case, slightly synonymous to the least squares method. Suppose that we have a 2-D plane and two vectors u⃗\vec{u} u and v⃗\vec{v} v. Our goal is to find a vector v^\hat{v}v^ such that v^\hat{v}v^ is the vector in the direction of v⃗\vec{v} v that is closest to u⃗\vec{u}u. Therefore, in order to minimize this distance, the vector u⃗−v⃗\vec{u} - \vec{v}u−v is orthogonal to v⃗\vec{v} v.

However, that means that v^\hat{v}v^ is, in fact, the projection of u⃗\vec{u} u onto v⃗\vec{v} v, typically denoted Projv⃗u⃗\textrm{Proj}_{\vec{v}}{\vec{u}}Projv​u. In fact, we have a formula for the projection vector:

v^=⟨u⃗,v⃗⟩⟨v⃗,v⃗⟩v⃗\hat{v} = \frac{\langle \vec{u}, \vec{v} \rangle}{\langle \vec{v}, \vec{v} \rangle} \vec{v}v^=⟨v,v⟩⟨u,v⟩​v

Let’s extend this thinking to more than one vector. Suppose we have a vector b⃗\vec{b} b as well as a set of vectors S={a1⃗,a2⃗,…,an⃗}S = \{ \vec{a_1}, \vec{a_2}, \dots , \vec{a_n} \} S={a1​​,a2​​,…,an​​} and we wish to find a vector b^\hat{b} b^ such that b^\hat{b} b^ is the vector closest to b⃗\vec{b} b and is also a linear combination of vectors from SS S. As an example, if SS S contains two vectors in 3-D space, the span of those two vectors would be a plane, and we’d want to find the closest vector within the span of the vectors of SS S such that that point on the plane is as close as possible to b⃗\vec{b} b. We can find that vector b^\hat{b} b^ by finding an orthogonal basis for SS S and projecting b⃗\vec{b} b onto those basis vectors, but that involves the Gram-Schmidt process and a series of computation-heavy calculations. Our solution is matrix algebra.


Matrix Approach to Least-Squares

Consider the matrix equation Ax⃗=b⃗A \vec{x} = \vec{b} Ax=b, where AA A is an m×nm \times n m×n matrix and x⃗\vec{x} x and b⃗\vec{b} b are nn n-tuples and mm m-tuples with real number entries, respectively. (Strictly speaking, x⃗\vec{x} x would be in Rn\mathbb{R}^n Rn and b⃗\vec{b} b would be in Rm\mathbb{R}^m Rm.) This equation does not always have a solution; If we were to row-reduce the augmented form of this matrix equation, it is possible that we end up with an inconsistent form. After all, our matrix equation simply represents a series of nn n linear equations, and if any of those equations are false, the whole system of equations would have no solution. We can, however, use an analogy to the above example to get as close a solution as we can to the true value of x⃗\vec{x} x by solving for the equation Ax^=b^A \hat{x} = \hat{b} Ax^=b^, where x^\hat{x} x^ is still an nnn-tuple but b^\hat{b} b^ is now the projection of b⃗\vec{b} b onto the column vectors of AA A.
It can be shown that x^\hat{x} x^ satisfies the following matrix equation:

A⊺Ax^=A⊺b⃗A^\intercal A \hat{x} = A^\intercal \vec{b}A⊺Ax^=A⊺b

where A⊺A^\intercal A⊺ represents the transpose of AA A. (The transpose of a matrix AA A is the matrix that has, as its rows, the columns of AA A.) While this fact seems to come from nowhere, it is relatively easy to prove.

Recall that Ax^=b^A \hat{x} = \hat{b} Ax^=b^, where b^\hat{b} b^ is the projection of b⃗\vec{b} b onto the column vectors of AA A. This means that this equation is consistent, since b⃗\vec{b} b is a linear combination of the column vectors of AA A. Also note that the minimum distance between any vector in the column space of AA A and b⃗\vec{b} b is the length of the vector orthogonal to b⃗\vec{b} b whose tail and head joins the two heads of b^\hat{b} b^ and b⃗\vec{b} b. This means that the vector b^−b⃗\hat{b} - \vec{b} b^−b is orthogonal to b^\hat{b} b^. Since b^\hat{b} b^ is simply a linear combination of the column vectors of AA A, which we shall call the vectors S=a1⃗,a2⃗,…,an⃗S = \vec{a_1}, \vec{a_2}, \dots , \vec{a_n} S=a1​​,a2​​,…,an​​. If aj⃗\vec{a_j} aj​​ is any one of these vectors, then, by the definition of orthogonality, aj⃗⋅(b⃗−Ax^)=0\vec{a_j} \cdot (\vec{b} - A \hat{x}) = 0aj​​⋅(b−Ax^)=0 and aj⃗⊺⋅(b⃗−Ax^)=0\vec{a_j}^\intercal \cdot (\vec{b} - A \hat{x}) = 0aj​​⊺⋅(b−Ax^)=0. (By the definition of the dot product.) Since each aj⃗⊺\vec{a_j}^\intercal aj​​⊺ is row of A⊺A^\intercal A⊺,

A⊺(b⃗−Ax^)=0⃗A^\intercal (\vec{b} - A \hat{x}) = \vec{0} A⊺(b−Ax^)=0

where 0⃗\vec{0} 0 is the zero vector, or a vector where each entry is 0. Distributing A⊺A^\intercal A⊺ and rearranging terms, we’ve shown that A⊺Ax^=A⊺b⃗A^\intercal A \hat{x} = A^\intercal \vec{b}A⊺Ax^=A⊺b. We can now use the fact in a number of applications, including linear regression and modeling lines of best fit.
